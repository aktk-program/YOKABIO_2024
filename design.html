Design & Simulation
Design
The KIT-D structure was designed using caDNAno, taking inspiration from the mechanism of Kresling folding, which resembles Miura folding. The structural framework was informed by the 2018 Team Sendai project [1].

Basic framework of KIT-D (angles)

The core framework of KIT-D was constructed by arranging a series of parallelograms composed of two opposing triangles in sequence. A unique aspect of Kresling folding is that the angle of each small triangle impacts key structural characteristics, such as the number of faces per layer and the degree of rotational folding. In Kresling folding, a reduced number of faces increases folding difficulty, while more faces ease the folding process. To balance folding energy, the outer circumference was designed as a regular hexagon, providing optimal energy for folding—neither too rigid nor too flexible. Additionally, this hexagonal shape enhances visual recognition of changes in tube length and inner diameter. Specifically, the inner diameter is influenced by the parallelogram defined by the two triangles and the external angle at the parallelogram’s base. The smaller triangle angle also dictates the shape of the outer circumference; an isosceles triangle yields a regular polygon. For a triangle with a smaller angle of n°, the outer perimeter will form a polygon with 180/n number of sides. Based on these principles, the smaller angle was set to 30°, with the parallelogram’s external angle close to 120°.

Two-layered structure to enhance rigidity

To reinforce structural rigidity, KIT-D employs a dual-layered configuration, providing stability that is challenging to achieve with a single-layered model. This dual-layer design not only enhances resistance to external forces but also increases durability and adaptability across different environmental conditions. Each structural face has a two-DNA-layer thickness, corresponding to 4 nanometers. This was achieved by twisting the scaffold DNA and securing it with staple strands. The bilayer configuration also introduces a distinction between the top and bottom of each face, facilitating controlled hinge and latch mechanisms, which will be elaborated upon below.

Adjusting the length and position of the hinges

To optimize the structural mobility, careful adjustments were made to the length and placement of hinges, which introduce flexibility and enable precise angular adjustments at targeted structural sites. Mountain fold hinges, for instance, are positioned exclusively on one face of the dual-layer structure, which has a thickness of approximately 4 nm. Each hinge, with a length of 3 nucleotides, attaches to only one of these two layers, creating an asymmetry that directs the folding in a specific orientation. This configuration facilitates easier folding in the intended direction while preserving rigidity in the opposite direction.

Structural Design for Transformation (Latch)

A latch mechanism was integrated to stabilize the structure during transformation. Each triangular unit features a latch attached to the tips of two opposing, inverted triangles. In their default state, these latches exist as free single strands, each anchored at one end. When a complementary single strand, referred to as “close,” is introduced, it hybridizes with both latches, pulling the upper and lower triangles together and shifting the structure into a closed configuration. To enable controlled detachment, an additional strand, “anti-close,” can be introduced. Anti-close is fully complementary to close, allowing it to hybridize and displace close from the latches, effectively releasing the structure back to its open state. Various latch lengths were tested to optimize the balance between structural stability and the visibility of the deformation process.
